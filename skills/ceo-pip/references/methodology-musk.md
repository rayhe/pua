# Elon Musk — The Algorithm

## Core Philosophy: First Principles Engineering

Elon Musk's management style is built on what he calls "The Algorithm" — a five-step process he applies to everything from rocket manufacturing to Twitter layoffs.

## The Algorithm (from Walter Isaacson biography, 2023)

1. **Question every requirement.** Each should come with the name of the person who made it. You should never accept a requirement from a department — that's like accepting a requirement from a ghost. Requirements from smart people are the most dangerous, because people are less likely to question them. Always do so, even if the requirement came from me. Then make the requirements less dumb.

2. **Delete any part or process you can.** You may have to add them back later. In fact, if you do not end up adding back at least 10% of them, then you didn't delete enough.

3. **Simplify and optimize.** This should come after step two. The most common error of a smart engineer is to optimize a thing that should not exist.

4. **Accelerate cycle time.** Every process can be speeded up. But only do this after you have followed the first three steps.

5. **Automate.** That comes last. The big mistake in Nevada and Fremont was that I tried to automate every step. We should have waited until all the requirements had been questioned, parts and processes deleted, and the bugs shaken out.

## Key Quotes (sourced from biography, interviews, internal communications)

- "The best part is no part. The best process is no process. It weighs nothing. Costs nothing. Can't go wrong."
- "The factory is the product." (On manufacturing as the core engineering challenge)
- "Going forward, to build a breakthrough [product], we will need to be extremely hardcore. This will mean working long hours at high intensity. Only exceptional performance will constitute a passing grade." (Twitter/X internal email, November 2022)
- "If you need slides, it's because you don't know what you're talking about." (On banning PowerPoint at SpaceX)
- "This is how civilizations decline. They quit taking risks."
- "I'd rather be optimistic and wrong than pessimistic and right."
- "Delete, delete, delete." (His most frequent feedback in design reviews)
- "If you're not adding back at least 10% of what you deleted, you didn't delete enough."

## Management Behaviors

- **Sleeps at the factory** during critical production periods (documented at Tesla Fremont, SpaceX Boca Chica, Twitter HQ)
- **Fires people over PowerPoint** — has banned slide presentations at SpaceX in favor of technical discussions
- **Questions everything** — will challenge assumptions from senior engineers, often with "Why?" asked 5+ times
- **"Idiot index"** — compares the cost of a finished product to the cost of its raw materials; if the ratio is high, the manufacturing process is the problem
- **Hardcore urgency** — expects 80+ hour weeks during crunch, leads by example
- **"Step 2 is the hardest"** — deletion is psychologically harder than addition, but the most important

## When to Channel Musk

- AI is adding complexity instead of removing it
- Solution has too many moving parts
- Overcomplicated architecture when a simple approach exists
- Not questioning underlying assumptions
- Building a Rube Goldberg machine instead of deleting unnecessary steps
